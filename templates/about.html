About me

Hello! Thank you for visiting my website. Its aim is to provide some interesting and thought provoking insights on the latest developments in climate change policies.

We are in a pivotal time for climate change. The science underpinning the need for urgent action is now generally accepted, although with some notable exceptions. In the words of Mark Carney, the UN envoy for climate change and former governor of the Bank of England:

“There is a growing international consensus that climate change is unequivocal. Many of the changes in our world since the 1950s are without precedent: not merely over decades but over millennia. Research tells us with a high degree of confidence that:

  • In the Northern Hemisphere the last 30 years have been the warmest since Anglo-Saxon times; indeed, eight of the ten warmest years on record in the UK have occurred since 2002;
  • Atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gases are at levels not seen in 800,000 years; and
  • The rate of sea level rise is quicker now than at any time over the last 2 millennia.

Evidence is mounting of man’s role in climate change. Human drivers are judged extremely likely to have been the dominant cause of global warming since the mid-20th century. While natural fluctuations may mask it temporarily, the underlying human-induced warming trend of two-tenths of a degree per decade has continued unabated since the 1970s.”

(Quote from speech given by Mark Carney to Lloyd’s of London 29 September 2015)

Although changes in the behaviour of individuals are important, this is dependent on many factors, some of which cannot be predicted (for example, the reduction in flights due to Covid 19). Therefore, I believe that behaviour changes need to be driven by thoughtful government policies and well drafted laws. During my career, I’ve worked with policy makers and regulators and I was appointed as an expert reviewer of the UN’s Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change’s Sixth Assessment Report, concentrating on the impact of the business sector on the fight against climate change.

As with any scientific issue, there is disagreement and debate about climate change. But modelling and research point to the probability that the acceleration of climate warming since the 1970s is man-made. Doing nothing to slow down this acceleration will have catastrophic consequences for the planet and its human population. So even if you are not convinced by the science, doing nothing is not an option. If there is a small chance that the projections are correct, can we afford not to do something about it?
